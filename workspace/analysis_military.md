## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The CENTCOM area of responsibility remains at **ELEVATED RISK** of military confrontation. Iran has pursued an asymmetric strategy combining direct military modernization with expanded proxy operations. US force posture maintains deterrence capability but faces challenges from dispersed threats and Iranian anti-access/area denial (A2/AD) developments.

**Key Judgments:**
- Iran's proxy network represents the most immediate threat to US interests and regional stability
- Gulf naval tensions remain high with significant risk of inadvertent escalation
- Iranian missile and drone capabilities have qualitatively improved, threatening regional bases
- Deterrence is strained but remains effective against major conflict

### 2.2 Proxy Capabilities by Theater

**Yemen (Houthis/"Ansar Allah"):**
- **Personnel:** 80,000-100,000 fighters
- **Iranian Support:** Missile components, drone technology, advisory personnel
- **Capabilities:**
  - Ballistic missiles (Burkan series, up to 1,200km)
  - Cruise missiles (Quds-2 based on Iranian design)
  - Loitering munitions (Samad drones)
  - Anti-ship missiles (C-801/802 derivatives)
- **Recent Activity:** Red Sea shipping attacks; missile strikes on Saudi Arabia/UAE
- **Assessment:** **HIGH THREAT** to maritime commerce; MEDIUM to US forces

**Lebanon (Hezbollah):**
- **Personnel:** 20,000-50,000 fighters; 150,000+ rockets/missiles
- **Iranian Support:** $700M-1B annually; advanced missiles; training
- **Capabilities:**
  - Rocket artillery (short to medium range)
  - Precision-guided missiles (increasing threat)
  - Anti-tank guided missiles (Kornet, etc.)
  - Air defense (limited)
  - Naval special operations
- **Assessment:** **SEVERE THREAT** to Israel; escalation tripwire

**Iraq (Shia Militia Groups):**
- **Groups:** Kataib Hezbollah, Asaib Ahl al-Haq, Badr Organization, etc.
- **Personnel:** 150,000+ (many integrated into Popular Mobilization Forces)
- **Iranian Support:** Arms, funding, training via Quds Force
- **Capabilities:**
  - Rockets (107mm, 122mm Katyusha, larger)
  - Armed drones (Ababil/Toufan series)
  - IED expertise
  - Intelligence networks
- **Recent Activity:** ~150 attacks on US forces since October 2023
- **Assessment:** **HIGH THREAT** to US personnel in Iraq/Syria

**Syria (Pro-Regime Militias):**
- **Personnel:** ~20,000 foreign fighters (Iraqi, Afghan, Pakistani, Lebanese)
- **Iranian Support:** Direct IRGC presence; weapons transfers to Hezbollah
- **Strategic Value:** Land bridge to Lebanon; forward missile bases
- **Assessment:** **MEDIUM THREAT**; consolidation of position

### 2.3 Proxy Warfare Assessment

**Strategic Value to Iran:**
1. **Plausible Deniability:** Attacks without direct attribution
2. **Cost Imposition:** Cheap weapons vs. expensive defenses
3. **Deterrence:** Threatens US partners (Saudi Arabia, Israel)
4. **Leverage:** Bargaining chips in negotiations
5. **Forward Defense:** Conflicts fought outside Iran

**US Counter-Proxy Challenges:**
- Attribution gaps complicate response decisions
- Proxy sanctuaries in urban areas (human shields)
- Risk of escalation to direct conflict
- Partner capacity limitations
- Domestic political constraints on sustained operations

## 3. GULF NAVAL ESCALATION RISKS

### 3.1 Strategic Maritime Environment

**Geographic Constraints:**
- Strait of Hormuz: 21 miles wide; 8-mile shipping channel
- ~21 million barrels/day transit (21% global consumption)
- Chokepoint vulnerability creates asymmetric advantage

**Iranian Naval Strategy:**
- **Anti-Access/Area Denial (A2/AD):** Missile batteries along coast; layered defense
- **Asymmetric Warfare:** Fast attack craft swarms; midget submarines; mines
- **Anti-Ship Missiles:** Chinese C-801/802; Iranian derivatives; coastal batteries
- **Mine Warfare:** 3,000-5,000 naval mines (contact, influence, drifting)

### 3.2 Scenario: Strait of Hormuz Closure

**Iranian Capabilities:**
- Could temporarily disrupt shipping via missile attacks, mines, small boat swarms
- Full closure unsustainable beyond weeks due to US military response
- Economic impact: Oil prices could spike 50-100%

**US Response Options:**
- Mine countermeasures (slow process)
- Suppression of Iranian coastal missile sites
- Convoy operations
- Alternative route promotion (SUMED pipeline, longer routes)

| 2021 | Mercer Street drone attack | MEDIUM |
| 2023 | Tanker seizures by IRGC | MEDIUM |

**Escalation Risk Assessment:**
- **Accidental Engagement:** MEDIUM - Close quarters in Hormuz, misidentification

## 4. DETERRENCE CREDIBILITY ASSESSMENT

### 4.1 Deterrence by Denial

**US Capabilities:**
- Missile defense systems deployed (PATRIOT, THAAD)
- Aegis ballistic missile defense (naval)
- Intelligence, surveillance, reconnaissance (ISR) dominance
- Air superiority
- **Assessment:** PARTIALLY EFFECTIVE - Protects bases but gaps exist; saturated attacks could overwhelm defenses

### 4.2 Deterrence by Punishment

**US Military Options:**
- Strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities
- Destruction of military infrastructure
- Naval blockade
- Decapitation strikes (high escalation risk)

**Iranian Cost-Benefit Calculus:**
- Regime survival paramount
- Fear of US regime change operations
- But: Belief US wants to avoid another Middle East war
- Time favors Iran (nuclear threshold, proxy entrenchment)

**Assessment:** Deterrence holds against existential attack but strains against limited strikes

### 4.3 Iranian Deterrence

**Against Israeli Attack:**
- Hezbollah rocket threat to Israeli cities
- Direct missile strikes from Iran
- Regional escalation through proxies

**Against US Attack:**
- Asymmetric retaliation (cyber, terrorism, proxies)
- Strait of Hormuz closure threat
- Missile attacks on regional bases

**Assessment:** Iranian deterrence credible against limited strikes; ambiguous against full-scale attack

### 5.2 Scenario Analysis: Limited Maritime Conflict

**Course of Action:**
1. US limited strikes on Yemen radar, missile launchers
2. Houthis expand targeting to US naval vessels
3. Iran increases support (denied); considers proxy activation in Iraq
4. Saudi Arabia/UAE pressured to join operations
5. De-escalation or expansion

**Assessment:** Containable but risk of mission creep; Iranian response likely asymmetric

### 5.3 Scenario Analysis: Israeli-Hezbollah War

**Trigger:** Cross-border attack; assassination; miscalculation

**Course of Action:**
1. Hezbollah rocket barrage on Israeli cities
2. Israeli air campaign against Lebanon
3. Iranian support to Hezbollah (arms, intelligence)
4. Potential Syrian front opening
5. Houthi/Iraqi militia attacks on US interests (solidarity)
6. US supports Israel; defends regional interests

**Assessment:** HIGH ESCALATION RISK - Could draw in regional powers; strain US resources

### 5.4 Scenario Analysis: Nuclear Crisis

**Trigger:** Iran enriches to 90% weapons-grade; Israeli preemptive strike

**Course of Action:**
1. Israeli air strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities
2. Iranian missile retaliation against Israel; regional US bases
3. US enters to defend interests; strikes on Iranian military
4. Proxy full activation
5. Strait of Hormuz threatened/closed

**Assessment:** MAJOR REGIONAL WAR - Destabilizes global economy; high casualties

## CONCLUSION

The military balance in the CENTCOM AOR favors the United States in conventional terms, but Iran's asymmetric capabilities—particularly proxy networks and missile forces—create significant challenges. Deterrence remains effective against existential threats but is tested daily by proxy harassment. The most likely conflicts involve proxy actors rather than direct state-on-state warfare, though miscalculation in the Gulf or Israeli-Hezbollah tensions could trigger wider escalation.

**Overall Risk Level: ELEVATED**

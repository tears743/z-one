**Date:** March 17, 2026

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The current US-Israel-Iran war (entering Week 3) has fundamentally altered the economic warfare landscape. Traditional sanctions analysis must now account for kinetic military operations that are disrupting Iran's economic lifelines more severely than any sanctions regime to date.

**Key Findings:**
1. **Sanctions effectiveness is being superseded by kinetic warfare** - bombing campaigns are achieving economic disruption faster than years of sanctions designations
2. **Iran's economic resilience mechanisms remain functional but severely strained** - Chinese and Russian evasion networks provide lifeline but at increasing cost
3. **Domestic economic pressure has not triggered regime change calculus** - instead appears to be hardening resistance and accelerating internal repression
4. **Humanitarian crisis risk is acute** - war has severed remaining humanitarian trade channels

## 1. SANCTIONS IMPACT ASSESSMENT

### 1.1 Traditional Sanctions Regime Effectiveness (Pre-War)

**Historical Impact (2018-2025):**
- Oil exports reduced from 2.5 million bpd to ~1.4 million bpd (44% reduction)
- Currency devaluation: ~70% against USD (official rates)
- Inflation sustained at 40-50% annually
- Banking isolation: Complete SWIFT exclusion achieved

**Assessment:** Sanctions achieved secondary objectives (economic isolation, revenue reduction) but failed primary strategic objective of compelling nuclear concessions or regime behavioral change.

### 1.2 War-Time Economic Warfare Impact (Current)

**Kinetic Economic Disruption:**
- Israeli strikes targeting Iranian economic infrastructure
- Hormuz maritime insecurity disrupting 20% of global oil shipments
- Insurance costs for Gulf shipping up estimated 300-500%
- Iranian oil export infrastructure under direct threat

**Quantified Impact:**
| Metric | Pre-War | Current (Est.) | Change |
|--------|---------|----------------|--------|
| Daily Oil Exports | ~1.4M bpd | <500K bpd | -64% |
| Hard Currency Reserves | ~$40B | <$25B (rapid depletion) | -37% |
| Import Capacity | Constrained | Severely Limited | -60% |
| Rial Stability | Weak | Collapsing | Critical |

### 1.3 Sanctions Evasion Network Resilience

**China's Role - Assessment: CRITICAL BUT COSTLY**
- Yuan-denominated purchases continue through shadow banking
- Estimated 60-70% of remaining Iranian oil exports flow to China
- Beijing extracting ~$12-18/bbl discount (vs. pre-war $8-10)
- Bank of Kunlun remains operational but transaction volumes limited

**Russia's Role - Assessment: EXPANDING**
- Moscow providing technical sanctions evasion expertise
- Potential ruble-rial clearing mechanism development
- Iran-Russia economic axis strengthening under shared pressure
- Barter trade (oil for weapons/technology) increasing

**UAE/Iraq Networks - Assessment: DISRUPTED**
- Traditional re-export hubs under direct Iranian missile threat
- UAE pivoting toward Israel/Gulf security alignment
- Iraqi Kurdish oil route disputes complicating overland alternatives

### 1.4 Sanctions Evasion Quantification

**Estimated Monthly Revenue Loss Prevention Through Evasion:**
- Chinese purchases: ~$1.2-1.8B/month (at discounted rates)
- Russian barter arrangements: ~$300-500M equivalent
- Smaller buyers (India, Syria): ~$200-300M/month
- **Total Evasion-Supported Revenue: ~$1.7-2.6B/month**

**Without evasion networks, Iranian state would face imminent fiscal collapse.**

## 2. OIL MARKET ANALYSIS

### 2.1 Pre-War Export Architecture

**Volume Breakdown (2025 averages):**
- China: 1.2-1.5 million bpd (85% of exports)
- India: 100,000-200,000 bpd (7%)
- Syria/Lebanon: 50,000-100,000 bpd (4%)
- Other: 50,000-100,000 bpd (4%)

**Pricing Dynamics:**
- Brent crude benchmark: ~$82-85/bbl
- Iranian discount to China: $8-12/bbl
- Effective Iranian realization: ~$70-77/bbl
- Transportation/insurance costs: Additional $3-5/bbl

### 2.2 War-Time Market Disruption

**Current Export Crisis:**
- Maritime insurance for Hormuz transits: Prohibitive or unavailable
- Ghost ship fleet under increased satellite surveillance
- Chinese "teapot" refineries facing pressure to reduce purchases
- Indian buyers pivoting to alternative suppliers

**Estimated Current Volumes:**
- March 2026 exports: <500,000 bpd (64% reduction from baseline)
- Revenue impact: ~$1.5-2.0B monthly loss
- Storage capacity reaching limits (floating storage increasing)

### 2.3 Global Market Impact

**Brent Price Response:**
- Pre-war: $82-85/bbl
- Current: Elevated due to Hormuz risk premium (estimated $95-105/bbl)
- Iranian supply loss contributing $8-12/bbl to global prices

**Strategic Petroleum Reserve Implications:**
- IEA members likely releasing reserves to offset Iranian disruption
- China potentially releasing strategic stocks to reduce import dependency
- Market adjusting to 1.4M bpd supply reduction

### 2.4 Future Scenarios

**Scenario A: Conflict Resolved Within 30 Days**
- Gradual export recovery to 1.0-1.2M bpd within 90 days
- Chinese buyers returning with increased discount demands
- Insurance markets gradually reopening

**Scenario B: Protracted Conflict (90+ Days)**
- Export infrastructure damage requiring months to repair
- Permanent loss of market share to Saudi Arabia, UAE, Iraq
- Iranian oil sector requiring massive Chinese investment to rebuild

**Scenario C: Regime Change/State Collapse**
- Complete export halt during transition period
- Unclear international recognition of successor government
- Potential UN/NATO administration of oil fields

## 4. POLICY EFFECTIVENESS EVALUATION

### 4.1 Strategic Objectives Assessment

**Primary Strategic Objectives of Economic Pressure:**

| Objective | Status | Assessment |
|-----------|--------|------------|
| Compel nuclear concessions | FAILED | Iran accelerating nuclear program |
| Trigger regime change | FAILED | Regime consolidating under Mojtaba Khamenei |
| Force diplomatic engagement | FAILED | War has replaced diplomacy |
| Degrade military capacity | PARTIAL SUCCESS | Economic constraints limiting procurement |
| Isolate Iran internationally | PARTIAL SUCCESS | China/Russia providing lifeline |

### 4.2 Hardening vs. Compliance Analysis

**Evidence of Hardening:**
1. **Leadership Succession:** Mojtaba Khamenei positioned as successor - no reformist opening
2. **Military Escalation:** Rather than economic concessions, Iran escalating to direct warfare
3. **Internal Repression:** 500+ espionage detentions show heightened security state
4. **Regional Proxy Activation:** Hezbollah, Houthis, Iraqi militias fully activated
5. **Nuclear Program:** Likely accelerating enrichment amid war

**Absence of Compliance Indicators:**
- No meaningful diplomatic outreach
- No nuclear program slowdown
- No prisoner releases (second American detained, not released)
- No regional de-escalation

### 4.3 Unintended Consequences

**Negative Externalities:**

1. **Humanitarian Crisis:**
   - Medicine shortages affecting civilian population
   - Food insecurity rising
   - Refugee flows to Turkey, Afghanistan

2. **Regional Economic Damage:**
   - UAE under missile attack (economic hub threatened)
   - Iraq oil exports disrupted
   - Turkey facing refugee costs and instability

3. **Global Economic Impact:**
   - Oil price spike affecting importing nations
   - Fertilizer supply concerns (Reuters analysis)
   - Shipping insurance costs increasing globally

4. **Great Power Polarization:**
   - China-Iran-Russia axis strengthening
   - Sanctions evasion technology proliferating
   - Alternative financial infrastructure development

### 4.4 Alternative Policy Assessment

**Current Approach: Maximum Pressure + Kinetic Action**
- Effectiveness: High economic damage, low political compliance
- Sustainability: Expensive for enforcing coalition
- Risks: Regional escalation, state collapse, humanitarian catastrophe

**Alternative Approaches Not Pursued:**

| Alternative | Pros | Cons |
|-------------|------|------|
| Negotiated Settlement | Ends conflict, verifiable nuclear limits | Requires sanctions relief, politically difficult |
| Containment Strategy | Lower cost, avoids war | Allows nuclear advancement, regional influence |
| Targeted Smart Sanctions | Focuses on regime elite | Less economic pressure, compliance uncertain |
| Humanitarian Trade Expansion | Reduces civilian suffering | May benefit regime, limited leverage |

### 4.5 Recommendations

**Tactical Recommendations:**

1. **Maintain Pressure on Chinese Purchases**
   - Secondary sanctions on Chinese banks processing Iranian oil payments
   - Target specific "teapot" refineries
   - Offer China alternative supply guarantees

2. **Disrupt Russian Economic Support**
   - Expand sanctions on Iran-Russia barter trade
   - Target shipping companies transporting Iranian oil to Russian ports
   - Cyber operations against ruble-rial clearing mechanisms

3. **Preserve Humanitarian Channels**
   - Re-establish SHTA/Swiss channel specifically for food/medicine
   - Allow limited banking access for UN humanitarian operations
   - Distinguish between regime pressure and civilian suffering

**Strategic Recommendations:**

1. **Define Clear Political Objectives**
   - Current: Regime change appears pursued without clear post-collapse plan
   - Alternative: Define limited objectives (nuclear rollback, regional behavior)

2. **Prepare for Post-War Economic Architecture**
   - Plan for Iranian oil re-entry to global markets
   - Design reconstruction financing mechanisms
   - Establish sanctions relief roadmap

3. **Address Root Causes of Evasion**
   - Coordinate with India on alternative supply arrangements
   - Work with China on energy security alternatives
   - Reduce global dependency on Iranian oil supply

## 6. CHINA & SANCTIONS EVASION: DETAILED EVALUATION

### 6.1 Chinese Strategic Calculus

**Why China Maintains Iranian Oil Purchases:**

1. **Energy Security:** Diversification away from Saudi/Gulf dependency
2. **Geopolitical Leverage:** Iran as counterweight to US regional influence
3. **Economic Opportunism:** Discounted oil ($12-18/bbl below market)
4. **Sanctions Evasion Technology Development:** Testing financial workaround infrastructure

### 6.2 Chinese Evasion Infrastructure

**Financial Channels:**
- Bank of Kunlun (CNPC subsidiary): Primary clearing bank
- Yuan-denominated settlement: Bypassing USD/SWIFT
- Shadow banking networks: Obscuring beneficial ownership
- Front companies in UAE, Iraq, Hong Kong: Facilitating trade documentation

**Maritime Infrastructure:**
- Ghost ship fleet: 200+ tankers operating without AIS
- Ship-to-ship transfers: Malaysian/Indonesian waters
- Chinese port calls: Limited direct Iranian tanker docking
- Insurance through Chinese state entities: Avoiding Western markets

### 6.3 Quantifying Chinese Evasion

**Estimated Annual Chinese Purchases:**
- Volume: 440 million barrels (1.2M bpd average)
- Price paid: ~$70/bbl (vs. $85 Brent)
- Discount value: $6.6B annual savings for China
- Total value: ~$30.8B annual trade

**Chinese Sanctions Exposure:**
- Secondary sanctions risk: HIGH for banks, MODERATE for refineries
- US leverage points: Technology exports, market access, Taiwan issue
- Likely Chinese response to pressure: Negotiate for alternative supply guarantees

### 6.4 Recommendations for Countering Chinese Evasion

1. **Secondary Sanctions:** Designate Chinese banks processing Iranian oil payments
2. **Technology Denial:** Restrict semiconductor/AI exports to Chinese refineries
3. **Alternative Supply:** Offer Saudi/UAE guarantees to replace Iranian barrels
4. **Intelligence Sharing:** Publicize Chinese evasion activities
5. **Diplomatic Pressure:** Link Taiwan/Iran issues in negotiations

## 7. CONCLUSIONS

### 7.1 Core Findings

1. **Economic pressure has achieved severe economic damage but failed to compel political compliance.** Iran's regime has chosen warfare over negotiation despite economic costs.

2. **China and Russia are providing sufficient economic lifeline to prevent rapid regime collapse** but not enough to restore economic stability.

3. **Domestic economic distress has not translated into regime-threatening unrest** due to wartime mobilization and severe internal repression.

4. **Humanitarian crisis is acute and worsening** with civilian population bearing disproportionate costs of economic warfare.

5. **Post-conflict economic reconstruction will require massive international investment** estimated at $200-500B over 10 years.

### 7.2 Strategic Outlook

**Short-term (0-6 months):**
- Economic collapse likely without significant external intervention
- Humanitarian catastrophe probable
- Regime survival dependent on Chinese/Russian support

**Medium-term (6-24 months):**
- Either negotiated settlement or state collapse
- Economic recovery possible with sanctions relief
- Reconstruction financing required

**Long-term (2-10 years):**
- Iran will remain economically significant regional power
- Oil sector recoverable with investment
- Sanctions architecture unlikely to return to pre-war levels

### 7.3 Key Uncertainties

1. **Chinese commitment level:** Will Beijing sustain support under secondary sanctions pressure?
2. **Military outcome:** Who controls Iranian territory and oil infrastructure post-war?
3. **Succession stability:** Can Mojtaba Khamenei consolidate power amid economic crisis?
4. **Global energy transition:** Will oil demand persist long enough for Iranian recovery?
